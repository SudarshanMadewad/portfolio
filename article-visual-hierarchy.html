WHY VISUAL HIERARCHY IS THE MOST UNDERRATED UX SKILL

You can pick any font and any colour and still build a bad interface. Here's what actually matters.

01 — THE PROBLEM NO ONE TALKS ABOUT

THE PROBLEM NO ONE TALKS ABOUT

Ask a junior designer what's wrong with a bad interface and they'll usually say: "The colours are off" or "The font doesn't look right."

Ask a senior designer the same question and they'll say: "I don't know where to look first."

That's visual hierarchy. And it's the difference between an interface that guides users and one that overwhelms them.

02 — WHAT VISUAL HIERARCHY ACTUALLY IS

WHAT VISUAL HIERARCHY ACTUALLY IS

Visual hierarchy is the order in which the human eye processes information on a screen.

It's not about making things pretty. It's about control — deciding what the user sees first, second, and third. And making sure that order matches what they actually need to do.

Every design decision you make — size, weight, colour, spacing, position — either reinforces or breaks that order.

03 — THE THREE LEVELS

THE THREE LEVELS

Think of every screen as having three levels of information:

Level 1 — What is this? The headline, the page title, the primary action. This is what the user reads in the first second. If this isn't clear, everything else fails.

Level 2 — What do I need to know? Supporting information, labels, descriptions. This is where users go after they understand Level 1 and want more context.

Level 3 — The details. Fine print, metadata, secondary actions. Users only read this when they've already committed to engaging.

The mistake most designers make is treating Level 2 and Level 3 information like Level 1. The result is a screen where everything shouts and nothing is heard.

04 — THE TOOLS YOU ALREADY HAVE

THE TOOLS YOU ALREADY HAVE

You don't need a new toolkit. You already have everything you need:

  • Size: Bigger = more important. Simple, but consistently underused.
  • Weight: Bold draws the eye before anything else.
  • Colour: High contrast = high importance. Low contrast = secondary.
  • Spacing: White space isn't empty — it's a signal.
  • Position: Top-left first, then across, then down.
05 — THE SQUINT TEST

THE SQUINT TEST

Here's the fastest way to check your own hierarchy:

Squint at your design until it blurs. What do you still see?

Those blurry shapes are what the user's brain registers in the first half-second — before they consciously read anything. If the wrong things are still visible when everything's blurry, your hierarchy is off.

It sounds too simple to work. Try it. You'll be surprised.

06 — THE REAL SKILL

THE REAL SKILL

Visual hierarchy isn't a technique. It's a habit of asking one question throughout the entire design process:

"What do I want the user to see next?"

Not "what looks good." Not "what's complete." Just — what's next?

When you design with that question in mind from the very first wireframe, the final product almost always feels clear and easy to use. Not because you added something clever, but because you took confusion away.

And that, more than any colour palette or font pairing, is what great UI design actually looks like.
