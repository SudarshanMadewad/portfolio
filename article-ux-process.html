01 — THE HONEST TRUTH ABOUT PROCESS

THE HONEST TRUTH ABOUT PROCESS

Most "design processes" you see online look perfect on a slide deck. In reality, they're messy, non-linear, and full of backtracking.

My process isn't perfect either. But after working across university platforms, creative agencies, and data dashboards — I've found a rhythm that consistently gets me from a fuzzy brief to something users actually want to use.

Here's exactly how I work, step by step.

02 — UNDERSTAND (BEFORE YOU DESIGN ANYTHING)

UNDERSTAND (BEFORE YOU DESIGN ANYTHING)

The biggest mistake junior designers make? Opening Figma before they understand the problem.

Before I touch any design tool, I spend time answering three questions:

  • Who is this for — and what do they actually need to do?
  • What does success look like for the business?
  • What's the one thing this design must get right?

I write these down as a simple brief — even if the client gave me one. Rewriting it in my own words forces me to find the gaps.

On the Manipal University project: The client said "redesign the homepage." But the real problem was that prospective students couldn't find programs fast enough and were bouncing. That reframe changed everything about how I approached the design.

03 — RESEARCH (BUT KEEP IT FOCUSED)

RESEARCH (BUT KEEP IT FOCUSED)

I don't do months of research. I do focused research that directly answers the questions I have.

For most projects I do two things:

Heuristic audit: I go through the existing product (if there is one) and mark everything that violates basic usability principles — hierarchy issues, unclear CTAs, confusing navigation, inconsistent patterns.

Competitor scan: I look at 3–5 similar products and note what they do well — not to copy, but to understand user expectations. Users come to your product with habits built from other products. Fighting those habits is always expensive.

The output is a list of clear problems and a sense of what users already expect.

04 — WIREFRAMES (THINK IN STRUCTURE, NOT BEAUTY)

WIREFRAMES (THINK IN STRUCTURE, NOT BEAUTY)

Wireframes are where I do my actual design thinking. Not in high-fidelity. Not in colour. Just boxes and text.

I use low-fidelity wireframes to answer layout questions:

  • What's the most important thing on this screen?
  • What does the user need to do next?
  • How much information is too much?

I share wireframes early and often — before anyone falls in love with a visual direction. It's much easier to move a box than to rebuild a polished component.

Rule I follow: If I can't explain the wireframe without talking through it — it needs to be simpler.

05 — HIGH FIDELITY (WHERE STRUCTURE MEETS FEELING)

HIGH FIDELITY (WHERE STRUCTURE MEETS FEELING)

Once the layout is locked, I move to high-fidelity in Figma.

This is where typography, colour, spacing, and micro-interactions come in. But by this point, the hard decisions are already made. High-fidelity is about execution — making the structure feel right, not figuring out what the structure should be.

Things I focus on at this stage:

  • Visual hierarchy — does the eye go where I want it to?
  • Consistency — are similar things treated the same way?
  • Edge cases — what does this look like with long text, empty states, or errors?
06 — TESTING (THE PART MOST DESIGNERS SKIP)

TESTING (THE PART MOST DESIGNERS SKIP)

Testing doesn't need to be a formal lab study. Even showing your design to 3 people who aren't designers tells you more than you'd expect.

I look for moments of hesitation. Where did they pause? Where did they click something they weren't supposed to? Where did they ask "what does this do?"

Those moments are gold. Each one is a design decision I got wrong — and now I know exactly what to fix.

Key mindset: Testing isn't about proving your design works. It's about finding where it doesn't — while it's still cheap to change.

07 — THE LOOP

THE LOOP

The process isn't linear. After testing, I usually go back to wireframes — or sometimes all the way back to understanding.

That's not failure. That's design working correctly.

The goal isn't to finish the process. The goal is to reach a design where the problems are gone and the user feels confident. However many loops it takes to get there — that's the right number.
